Key Definitions

Click any topic to expand. Learn the term, definition, and example together.

TermDefinitionExample
Market FailureWhen the free market fails to allocate resources efficiently, resulting in a net welfare loss — the socially optimal output Q* is not produced.Pollution; public goods not provided; merit goods underconsumed
Allocative EfficiencyAchieved when P = MC — resources are allocated such that it is impossible to make one person better off without making another worse off (Pareto optimal).Long-run competitive equilibrium where P = MC
Productive EfficiencyOutput is produced at the lowest possible average cost — on the minimum point of the LRAC curve. No unnecessary waste of resources.LR equilibrium in perfect competition: P = min AC
Dynamic EfficiencyResources are allocated efficiently over time — new products and production methods are developed through R&D investment financed by retained profit.Schumpeter: monopoly profit funds innovation ("creative destruction")
Deadweight Welfare LossThe loss of total economic surplus (consumer + producer surplus) when output deviates from the socially optimal level Q*. Shown as a triangle on a diagram.Triangle between Qm and Q* on any externality or monopoly diagram
Pareto EfficiencyA state where no reallocation of resources can make one person better off without making at least one person worse off. The theoretical benchmark for optimal allocation.Competitive equilibrium where P = MC for all goods simultaneously
Saying "an externality is a cost or benefit to a third party" is not sufficient. You must state the divergence between private and social costs/benefits AND the resulting welfare loss.
TermDefinitionExample
ExternalityA cost or benefit falling on a third party not involved in the transaction, causing a divergence between private and social costs/benefits and resulting in market failure and a welfare loss.CO₂ emissions (negative production); vaccination herd immunity (positive consumption)
Negative Production ExternalityMSC > MPC. Third parties bear costs from firms' production. The free market overproduces at Qm > Q*, creating a deadweight welfare loss.Steel factory releasing sulphur dioxide into a river
Negative Consumption ExternalityMSB < MPB. Third parties bear costs from individuals' consumption. The free market overconsumes at Qm > Q*, creating a welfare loss.Cigarette smoking — costs fall on NHS and passive smokers
Positive Production ExternalityMSC < MPC. Third parties benefit from firms' production. The free market underproduces at Qm < Q*, creating a welfare loss below the social optimum.Pharmaceutical R&D — knowledge spillovers benefit other firms and consumers
Positive Consumption ExternalityMSB > MPB. Third parties benefit from individuals' consumption. The free market underconsumes at Qm < Q*, creating a welfare loss.Vaccination — herd immunity benefits the non-vaccinated
MPC (Marginal Private Cost)The cost of producing one additional unit borne only by the producer. Excludes all external costs. Represented by the supply curve in a free market.Labour, raw materials, energy costs for a factory
MSC (Marginal Social Cost)The full cost to society of one additional unit: MSC = MPC + MEC (Marginal External Cost). Includes all externalities.Factory MPC + pollution cost imposed on local residents
MPB (Marginal Private Benefit)The benefit to the individual consumer of one additional unit. Excludes all external benefits. Represented by the demand curve.Utility gained by a smoker from one cigarette
MSB (Marginal Social Benefit)The full benefit to society of one additional unit: MSB = MPB + MEB (Marginal External Benefit). Includes all externalities.Vaccination benefit to individual + herd immunity externality to wider population
Socially Optimal Output (Q*)The output where MSB = MSC — maximises total welfare. Any output above or below Q* creates a deadweight welfare loss. This is the government's policy target.Output achieved under a correctly-set corrective tax or subsidy
Do not write "the market underprovides public goods." The market provides ZERO — it is a complete market failure, not partial.
TermDefinitionExample
Public GoodA good that is simultaneously non-excludable AND non-rival. The free market supplies zero of this good — a complete market failure due to the free rider problem.National defence, flood defences, street lighting, lighthouses
Non-ExcludableOnce the good is provided it is impossible or prohibitively costly to prevent non-payers from consuming it — the price mechanism cannot function.Cannot exclude a non-taxpayer from protection provided by the army
Non-RivalConsumption by one person does not reduce the quantity or quality available for others — the marginal cost of an additional user is zero.One person watching a firework display does not reduce anyone else's enjoyment
Free Rider ProblemBecause non-payers cannot be excluded, individuals have no incentive to pay voluntarily — private firms cannot recover costs and so market provision is zero.No private firm can profitably supply national defence
Private GoodA good that is both excludable and rival — can be efficiently allocated by the free market price mechanism.A sandwich, a car, a cinema ticket
Quasi-Public GoodA good with some but not all characteristics of a public good — partially non-excludable or becomes rival under congestion.Roads (non-excludable but rival when congested); national parks
TermDefinitionExample
Information FailureWhen consumers or producers have inaccurate, incomplete, or asymmetric information, leading to sub-optimal decisions and market failure. Causes under- or overconsumption relative to Q*.Consumers underestimate long-run health risks of smoking; food market misinformation
Asymmetric InformationWhen one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other — can lead to adverse selection or moral hazard.Insurance markets (insurer knows less than policyholder); second-hand car market (Akerlof's lemons)
Adverse SelectionWhen asymmetric information before a transaction causes a market to disproportionately attract high-risk participants, worsening average transaction quality.High-risk individuals more likely to purchase insurance at any given price
Moral HazardWhen asymmetric information after a transaction causes one party to take greater risks, as the costs are borne by another.Fully insured drivers may take less care; bailed-out banks may take on excessive risk
Merit GoodA good that is underprovided and underconsumed by the free market because consumers undervalue its true long-run benefit, due to imperfect information. MPB < MSB — market produces Qm < Q*.Education, healthcare — individuals underestimate long-run returns; high-quality student accommodation
Demerit GoodA good that is overprovided and overconsumed because consumers underestimate its true harm, due to imperfect information. MPB > MSB — market produces Qm > Q*.Tobacco, alcohol, gambling, unhealthy foods
TermDefinitionExample
Corrective Tax (Pigouvian)A per-unit tax set equal to the MEC at Q*. Shifts MPC up to MSC, reducing output from Qm to Q* and eliminating the deadweight welfare loss.UK carbon price; cigarette excise duty (~£4.40/pack)
Corrective SubsidyA per-unit subsidy set equal to the MEB at Q*. Shifts the effective supply or demand to close the gap between private and social optima, raising output from Qm to Q*.NHS funding; state education; R&D tax credits
Maximum Price (Ceiling)A legal upper limit set below equilibrium Pe. Improves affordability but creates excess demand (shortage = Qd − Qs) and may cause black markets and reduced quality.Rent controls; energy price caps; student accommodation rent caps (Scotland)
Minimum Price (Floor)A legal lower limit set above equilibrium Pe. Protects producers but creates excess supply (surplus = Qs − Qd).National Living Wage; EU Common Agricultural Policy price supports
Tradeable Permits (ETS)Cap-and-trade: a total cap on pollution is set. Firms buy/sell permits — creates a market price for carbon and incentivises least-cost abatement.UK ETS; EU ETS (launched 2005, reformed 2018)
Regulation / LegislationDirect rules — mandatory standards, bans, or disclosure requirements. Does not require precise knowledge of MEC.Ban on leaded petrol; advertising restrictions on junk food aimed at children
State ProvisionDirect government supply financed by taxation. Used for public goods (zero private provision) and merit goods (sub-optimal private provision).NHS, state education, national defence, police, fire service
Information ProvisionGovernment campaigns or mandatory labelling to correct information failure and improve consumer decision-making.Cigarette health warnings; food nutritional labelling; anti-drug campaigns
Nudge (Behavioural Economics)A policy altering the choice architecture to steer behaviour without restricting options or using financial incentives — exploits behavioural biases (present bias, status quo bias, loss aversion).Opt-out pension auto-enrolment; placing fruit at eye level in canteens
Government FailureWhen government intervention produces a net welfare loss — the cure is worse than the disease. The intervention makes total welfare worse than the original market failure.Over-allocated ETS permits; unintended consequences of rent controls; regulatory capture
Regulatory CaptureWhen regulators are dominated by the interests of the firms they regulate, causing them to act against the public interest they were designed to protect.Financial regulators becoming too close to banks in the period before the 2008 crisis
TermDefinitionExample
Perfect CompetitionMany buyers/sellers, homogeneous products, perfect information, free entry/exit. Firms are price takers (P = AR = MR). LR equilibrium: P = MC = min AC — allocatively and productively efficient.Agricultural commodity markets (wheat, sugar)
MonopolySingle firm with significant market power; price maker. Profit-maximised at MR = MC → Qm < Qc, Pm > MC — allocatively inefficient. Earns supernormal profit in LR.Royal Mail (letters), Network Rail, water companies
OligopolyDominated by a few large firms. High interdependence — firms must consider rivals' reactions. Non-price competition common. Kinked demand curve explains price rigidity.UK supermarkets (Tesco, Sainsbury's, ASDA, Morrisons); mobile networks
Monopolistic CompetitionMany firms selling differentiated products. Some pricing power from differentiation. Free entry → normal profit in LR. Allocatively inefficient (P > MC) but some product variety.Restaurants, hairdressers, clothing retailers, broadband providers
Contestable MarketA market with low barriers to entry and exit. Threat of potential entry keeps incumbent firms from earning supernormal profit even without actual competition (Baumol's theory).Airlines on routes where aircraft can be redeployed quickly
Supernormal ProfitProfit above the minimum required to keep a firm in the industry: TR > TC including opportunity cost. Sustained in LR only where barriers to entry exist.A monopolist sheltered by patents, network effects, or high capital requirements
Normal ProfitThe minimum profit required to keep a firm in the industry in the long run — the opportunity cost of capital and entrepreneurship. AR = AC in LR competitive equilibrium.LR equilibrium in perfect competition and monopolistic competition
Price DiscriminationCharging different prices to different consumers for the same good, not based on cost differences. Requires: market power, ability to segment markets, prevention of resale.3rd degree: student/senior rail fares; peak vs off-peak pricing; broadband new vs existing customers
Consumer SurplusThe difference between consumers' willingness to pay and the price actually paid — the area above the market price and below the demand curve.Triangle above P* below the demand curve, from 0 to equilibrium quantity
X-Inefficiency (Leibenstein)Productive inefficiency within a firm due to lack of competitive pressure — actual costs are higher than the minimum possible because management has no incentive to minimise them.Nationalised industries or monopolies sheltered from competition
TermDefinitionExample
PED% change in Qd ÷ % change in price. Always negative (inverse relationship). |PED| > 1: elastic. |PED| < 1: inelastic. Factors: availability of substitutes, necessity vs luxury, time period, proportion of income.Cigarettes: inelastic (~−0.4) — tax raises revenue but barely reduces Qd
PES% change in Qs ÷ % change in price. Always positive. Factors: time period (SR vs LR), spare capacity, ease of factor substitution, storability.Student accommodation: highly inelastic PES — new buildings take years to construct
YED% change in Qd ÷ % change in income. Positive for normal goods, negative for inferior goods. YED > 1 for luxury goods, 0 < YED < 1 for necessities.Positive YED for restaurants; negative YED for supermarket own-brand basics
XED% change in Qd of good A ÷ % change in price of good B. Positive for substitutes (XED > 0); negative for complements (XED < 0).Positive XED: butter and margarine (substitutes); negative XED: cars and petrol (complements)

Chain of reasoning to write in full
  1. Firms only account for MPC (internal costs), ignoring the MEC — the cost imposed on third parties such as local communities or the environment per unit of output.
  2. The free market equilibrium is at Qm (where MPC = MPB = D). This exceeds Q* (where MSC = MSB) — the market overproduces by Qm − Q* units.

Four critical points for exam answers
  1. The tax must equal the MEC at Q*, NOT at Qm. Setting the tax at Qm is the single most common diagram error in externality questions.
  2. The tax shifts the supply curve leftward from S₁ (MPC) to S₂ (MPC + tax = MSC). The new market equilibrium is where S₂ meets the demand curve: output Q*, price P*.
  3. The original deadweight welfare loss from overproduction is eliminated. The market now produces the socially optimal quantity.
  4. Tax revenue = tax per unit × Q* — this can be used to fund subsidies for clean alternatives (double dividend argument).
Draw the tax as the vertical gap between S₁ and S₂ at Q*, not at Qm. The gap is larger at Qm — that's wrong.

What to say about this diagram
  1. The monopolist maximises profit at MR = MC, giving output Qm and price Pm. Because the demand curve slopes downward, MR lies below AR throughout — so at profit-maximising output, Pm > MR = MC. Price exceeds marginal cost: allocatively inefficient.
  2. The DWL triangle between Qm and Qc represents the net loss of total surplus. Consumer surplus is partly transferred to the monopolist as supernormal profit (rectangle) and partly destroyed entirely (the DWL triangle).
  3. However: the monopolist may achieve productive efficiency through economies of scale (lower AC). Supernormal profit may fund R&D — dynamic efficiency (Schumpeter's creative destruction). This is your evaluation point.

Key evaluation points for essay answers
  1. A maximum price must be set below Pe to be binding — if set above Pe it has no effect. The further below Pe it is set, the larger the shortage.
  2. Unintended consequences: black markets may emerge; landlords/suppliers may exit the market if returns are too low; quality may fall as sellers find other ways to adjust.
  3. Effectiveness depends on: elasticity of supply (inelastic supply → smaller shortage); enforcement capacity; how far below equilibrium the cap is set.
  4. In student accommodation context (2024 paper): reduces rent affordability problem for those who access housing, but risks reducing total supply if landlords exit the market.

Key Economic Arguments

Pre-built KAA chains and evaluation points — slot into any essay.

FOR Intervention

When production or consumption generates negative externalities, the free market overproduces because private agents only account for MPC, ignoring the MEC imposed on third parties. Since MSC > MPC, the market equilibrium (where MPC = MPB) produces at Qm > Q* (where MSC = MSB), creating a deadweight welfare loss. A Pigouvian tax equal to the MEC at Q* shifts MPC up to MSC, reducing output to Q* and eliminating the DWL — restoring allocative efficiency.

Negative externality → MSC > MPC → free market Qm > Q* → overproduction → DWL → corrective tax = MEC at Q* → MPC + tax = MSC → new equilibrium at Q* → DWL eliminated → allocative efficiency restored
💡 UK carbon price (~£45/tonne); cigarette excise duty (~£4.40/pack)

Public goods are simultaneously non-excludable and non-rival. Non-excludability means no private firm can prevent free riders from consuming the good without paying — the price mechanism cannot operate. The free rider problem causes market provision to be zero (not just insufficient — zero). Only the government, financed through general taxation, can ensure these goods are supplied.

Non-excludable + non-rival → free rider problem → impossible to charge a price → zero private profit motive → market provision = 0 → complete market failure → government provision via taxation required
💡 National defence (~£50bn UK 2024); flood defences; street lighting

Due to imperfect information, consumers underestimate the long-run benefits of merit goods such as education, healthcare, and high-quality housing. MPB < MSB — they underconsume at Qm < Q*, creating a deadweight welfare loss. Government subsidies lower the effective price, raising consumption towards Q*. State provision eliminates the price barrier entirely.

Imperfect information → consumers undervalue long-run benefits → MPB < MSB → free market Qm < Q* → underconsumption → DWL → subsidy = MEB at Q* → effective price falls → consumption rises to Q* → DWL eliminated
💡 NHS (free at point of use); state education (100% subsidised); vaccination programmes

A monopolist maximises profit at MR = MC, producing Qm and setting Pm. Since Pm > MC, the monopolist is allocatively inefficient — consumers value the last unit at Pm (more than cost MC), yet it is not produced. This creates a DWL triangle between Qm and Qc. Consumer surplus is redistributed as supernormal profit, worsening equity. CMA regulation or price caps can push price towards MC.

Monopolist MR = MC → Qm < Qc → Pm > MC → P > MC → allocative inefficiency → DWL → consumer surplus redistributed as profit → equity worsened → CMA regulation/price cap → P → MC → DWL reduced

AGAINST / Evaluation Points

Government intervention can generate net welfare losses exceeding the original market failure. The government cannot accurately measure MEC or MEB — no market exists to reveal these values — so a corrective tax or subsidy set at the wrong level may under- or over-correct. Regulation creates unintended consequences (e.g. rent controls reduce housing supply and quality; ETS over-allocation of permits collapsed the carbon price 2009–13). Regulatory capture causes regulators to serve firms rather than consumers. Time lags mean policies arrive after conditions have changed.

Government lacks MEC data → tax set incorrectly → over- or under-correction → intervention may create a net welfare loss exceeding the original market failure → government failure
🔑 Key question: is the government failure smaller or larger than the original market failure? Depends on data quality and regulatory independence.

Effectiveness of a corrective tax depends critically on the PED for the taxed good. If PED is inelastic (as typical for addictive goods like tobacco), a tax raises price substantially but produces only a small fall in quantity — the externality is barely corrected, though revenue rises sharply. Furthermore, such taxes are regressive: lower-income households spend a higher proportion of income on these goods, so the tax burden falls disproportionately on them — worsening income distribution.

Inelastic PED → tax raises price → small fall in Qd → externality barely reduced → but tax revenue rises → regressive burden on lower-income households → equity worsened → efficiency-equity trade-off
🔑 UK data: cigarette duty ~£4.40/pack, yet ~13% of UK adults still smoke — consistent with inelastic PED for tobacco.

While a monopolist is statically allocatively inefficient (Pm > MC), supernormal profit provides resources for R&D and innovation. Schumpeter's theory of "creative destruction" argues that the prospect of monopoly profit is precisely what incentivises innovation — firms invest to gain a temporary monopoly position. This dynamic efficiency may outweigh the static DWL from Pm > MC. Additionally, a monopolist may achieve economies of scale unavailable to competitive firms.

Supernormal profit → funds R&D → new products/processes → dynamic efficiency → long-run consumer benefit and lower costs → may outweigh static DWL from Pm > MC
🔑 Counter: X-inefficiency (Leibenstein) — monopolists lacking competitive pressure may let costs rise rather than invest in R&D (managerial slack). 💡 Pharmaceutical patents: 20-year monopoly protection necessary to recover ~£1bn+ R&D cost per new drug

Essay Framework

Exact structure for Section B. The 25-mark question requires KAA + evaluation + supported conclusion throughout.

AQA Level 5 (21–25 marks) requires "supported evaluation throughout the response AND in a final conclusion." Evaluation only at the end keeps you at Level 3–4.
INTRO

Introduction — 3–4 sentences max

Define 2–3 key terms directly from the question. Signal the direction of your argument. Do not state your conclusion yet and do not waste time on irrelevant background.

e.g. "A negative externality arises when MSC exceeds MPC, causing the free market to overproduce at Qm > Q* and creating a deadweight welfare loss. This essay will assess whether a corrective tax is the most effective policy response..."

KAA1

KAA Point 1 — argument + full chain + diagram

State the economic argument. Build a 3+ step causal chain from cause to mechanism to welfare effect. Draw and fully label a diagram. Reference it explicitly: "As shown in Figure 1, the market overproduces at Qm..."

Diagram must include: both axes labelled, all curves named, Qm, Q*, Pm, P*, DWL triangle shaded.

EVAL1

Evaluation 1 — challenge KAA1 directly

Do not start a new argument — challenge the one you just made using conditionality, magnitude, time period, or conflicting evidence. Name the specific economic mechanism that limits the argument.

e.g. "However, the effectiveness of this corrective tax depends critically on the PED for the good. Cigarettes have a PED of approximately −0.4 — highly inelastic — which means a tax raises price substantially but reduces quantity consumed only marginally, barely correcting the externality..."

KAA2

KAA Point 2 — second distinct argument

A separate, distinct economic argument. Its own chain of reasoning. Its own diagram if the concept is different. Do not restate KAA1 in different words.

EVAL2

Evaluation 2 — second distinct evaluative point

Ideally at a different level of analysis from EVAL1: e.g. EVAL1 challenges the mechanism, EVAL2 challenges the policy (government failure, equity trade-off, dynamic vs static efficiency).

CONCL

Conclusion — prioritised, conditional judgement

Explicitly state which argument is MOST important and WHY in the specific context of this question. Use conditional language. Do not introduce new arguments. This is what pushes you to Level 5.

e.g. "On balance, the case for a corrective tax is strongest when the MEC is large and measurable. However, in practice — particularly for demerit goods with inelastic demand — a combination of taxes and information provision is likely to be more effective than either alone, since taxes alone fail to correct the underlying information failure that drives overconsumption."

The 15-mark question says "Explain…" — it requires KAA only. Evaluation is not on the mark scheme. Write 2–3 well-developed analytical points.

1

Point 1 — argument + chain + diagram if applicable

Define the key term(s). Build a 3+ step chain. Draw a diagram if the concept requires one (externalities, market structures, cost curves). Reference it explicitly.

2

Point 2 — second distinct argument

Separate, fully developed point. At the top of Level 3, analysis must be clear and complete — not half-developed or contradicted by your next sentence.

Do not write evaluation in the 15-mark question. The mark scheme does not award it and you need that time for the 25-mark essay.

1

Chain of reasoning — never stop at one step

Every argument must go 3+ steps deep. "Negative externality leads to market failure" earns Level 2 at best. The full chain: "MSC > MPC → firms produce at MPC = MPB → Qm > Q* → at units between Q* and Qm, MSC > MSB → net welfare loss → DWL = triangle between Q* and Qm."

2

Diagram quality — every label, every time

Both axes. All curves named. Both equilibria marked. Both prices. Both quantities. DWL shaded. Every unlabelled element is a mark left on the table.

3

Specific evaluation — name the mechanism

Never write "however, there are other factors." Start with the mechanism: "However, the effectiveness of this policy depends on the PED of cigarettes (approximately −0.4 in the UK), which means a tax raises price substantially but reduces quantity only marginally — the externality is barely corrected."

4

Prioritised, conditional conclusion

State which argument is stronger and why in the specific context of the question. "On balance..." is fine but must be followed by a specific reason. An unsupported conclusion keeps you at Level 3.

5

Technical vocabulary throughout

MPC, MSC, MPB, MSB, MEC, MEB, Q*, Qm, deadweight welfare loss, allocative efficiency, Pareto optimal, price elasticity of demand. Never say "supply", "costs", "benefits" when you mean MPC, MEC, MEB. The mark scheme awards marks for correct use of economic terminology.
